What is the Purpose of our Database?


What Are Bacteriophages?

Bacteriophages are viruses that infect bacteria. Bacteriophages, or phages, are nearly infinite in our world and due to their abundancy, there is a lack of knowledge regarding these microscopic particles. Phages cannot reproduce by themselves, but they can reproduce within a host cell, or a bacterium. Phages are non-living, but possess the essential hereditary material to reproduce within a host. This genetic material is protected by a crystalline envelope called the "head" of a phage, or "capsid."

Phages can undergo two types of life cycles, lytic and lysogenic:

In the lytic cycle, the phage is in a "productive" phase where the host cell machinery expresses the phage genes. Therefore, coat proteins are assembled with replicated phage genomes at extremely high rates, eventually leading to lysis of the host cell. These newly produced phage particles then go on to infect new bacterial cells.

In the lysogenic cycle, phages undergo a more "passive" or "dormant" phase. The phage genome becomes integrated into the host genome and remains dormant. These integrated genomes are also known as prophages. Phage genomes can remain within the host genome for many generations without disturbance. However, the phage genome eventually gets expressed, usually due to some stimulation of expression by stress factors or other natural causes. Once gene expression occurs, the phage genome then enters the lytic cycle.

What We Are Doing

Students are encouraged to undergo a year-long research project to discover bacteriophages. While viruses, specifically bacteriophages, are some of the most diverse and abundant organisms on the planet, there is still a lot of unknown information about them. The research conducted through the IWU SEA-Lab program aims at gaining an understanding of the genetic makeup of viruses. Specifically, the program focuses on discovering bacteriophages isolated from soil/water samples in the environment. Each bacteriophage has a range of hosts in which they infect. Some bacteriophages may infect only one host, while others may infect many. The purpose of using several hosts during discovery is to:

  • Increase our range of knowledge on bacteriophages in general
  • Contribute to the discovery of a broader range of bacteriophages
  • Investigate a single bacteriophage's host range

While the SEA-PHAGES program mainly focuses on finding viruses that infect the Mycobacteria and other hosts within the phylum Actinobacteria, there is not a database for IWU students to keep information pertaining to bacteriophages that infect Rhodobacter capsulatus and other related bacterial hosts.

The SEA-PHAGES Program


What is it?

SEA-PHAGES (Science Education Alliance-Phage Hunters Advancing Genomics and Evolutionary Science) is a two-semester, discovery-based undergraduate research course that begins with simple digging in the soil or extracting water samples from the environment to find new viruses, but progresses through a variety of microbiology techniques and eventually to complex genome annotation and bioinformatic analyses.

The program aims to increase undergraduate interest and retention in the biological sciences through immediate immersion in authentic, valuable, yet accessible research. By finding and naming their own bacteriophages, students develop a sense of project ownership and have a ready-made personal research project at a fraction of the cost of traditional apprentice-based research programs. Some of the positive effects of the SEA-PHAGES program have been reported here.

SEA-PHAGES is jointly administered by Graham Hatfull's group at the University of Pittsburgh and the Howard Hughes Medical Institute's Science Education division.

How IWU Joined

Illinois Wesleyan University joined the SEA-PHAGES program in 2011 as an Associate member of Cohort IV.
